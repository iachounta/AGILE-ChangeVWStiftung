Choosing an AI tool

How to choose an appropriate AI tool for your classroom

Instruction sheet #01

A decision guide for K-12 teachers: match your learning objective, school level, subject, and applicable regulations to the right AI tool — before you start.

1
Decision helper — find your tool match ▼

Answer the four questions below to receive a tailored recommendation.

2
What to look for in any AI tool ▼
Age appropriateness. Check the minimum age in the tool's terms of service. Most general-purpose AI tools require users to be 13 or 18. For primary school, use education-specific platforms designed for younger learners.
Data privacy and GDPR compliance. Avoid tools requiring student accounts unless a Data Processing Agreement is in place. Never enter student names or identifying information.
Content moderation. Does the tool have safeguards against inappropriate content? Test general-purpose tools carefully before use with students.
Transparency and explainability. Prefer tools that do not present AI-generated content as factual without caveats.
Cost and access equity. Will all students access the tool equally? Consider free vs. paid tiers and accessibility requirements.
Tip

Test any tool yourself for at least 30 minutes before use with students. If safeguards fail in testing, do not use it.

3
Relevant regulations and frameworks ▼

These frameworks apply across European school systems and should inform your tool choice.

EU AI Act (2024)
AI systems in education are classified as high-risk. Providers must ensure transparency, human oversight, and accuracy.
GDPR
Personal data of minors requires parental consent or a school-level DPA. Prefer EU-based data storage.
Council of Europe AI guidelines
Recommends human oversight and transparency. Teachers retain full pedagogical responsibility.
School / national policy
Always check your national ministry website and your school's IT/data policy before use.
Important

If no approved tool list exists, consult your school leadership or data protection officer before using any AI tool with students.

4
Pre-use checklist ▼
0 of 7 completed
  • I have checked my school's approved tool list or consulted my data protection officer
  • The tool meets the minimum age requirement for my students
  • I have verified GDPR / data privacy compliance — no student personal data is required
  • I have tested the tool myself and it is appropriate for my subject and level
  • All students can access the tool equally — free tier or school licence available
  • I have a plan to maintain teacher oversight during student use
  • I can explain to students at a basic level what the tool does and does not do

Using generative AI for lesson planning

Instruction sheet #03

A step-by-step guide for teachers: how to use AI tools to support — not replace — your lesson planning process, with a built-in prompt builder.

School level
K-12 (all levels)
Time needed
15 – 30 min
Prerequisite
Sheet #01 completed
1
Before you open any AI tool ▼
Write your learning objective first. Complete: "By the end of this lesson, students will be able to…" before opening the AI tool. The objective guides everything.
Have your context ready. Subject, grade, lesson duration, curricular constraints, and students' prior knowledge. The more specific, the more useful the AI output.
Confirm your tool is approved. Check your school's approved tool list (see Sheet #01). Do not enter student names, photos, or identifying information.
Tip

If you cannot clearly state your learning objective before using the AI, the lesson plan it produces will be generic. Invest 5 minutes here — it saves 20 later.

2
Writing your prompt — and a builder to help ▼
A good prompt has three parts: (1) role — tell the AI it is helping a teacher; (2) task — what you want it to produce; (3) context — subject, grade, duration, and learning objective.
Example prompt structure:
"You are helping a [subject] teacher at [school level]. Create a [duration]-minute lesson plan on [topic] for [grade/age]. Learning objective: students will be able to [objective]. Include: [starter / main activity / exit ticket / other]."

Tip — iterating

If the first output is too generic, refine: "Make the starter activity more hands-on" or "Adapt this for students with lower reading levels." Save prompts that work well for reuse.

3
Reviewing and adapting the output ▼
Never use AI output without review. AI-generated plans may contain factual errors or activities unsuitable for your students. You are responsible for everything in your classroom.
Check curriculum alignment. Does the activity match your requirements? Is the cognitive level appropriate and timing realistic?
Add your professional knowledge. Insert your own examples and knowledge of your students. The AI has no knowledge of your class — you do.
Ask the AI to explain its choices. Prompt: "Why did you suggest this activity for this age group?" and decide whether to keep, modify, or discard it.
Important

Do not copy AI output directly into official documents without significant review. Treat AI output as a first draft, not a finished product.

4
Pre-lesson checklist ▼
0 of 6 completed
  • I defined my learning objective before using the AI tool
  • No student personal data was entered into the AI tool
  • I reviewed all AI-generated content for factual accuracy
  • Activities align with my curriculum requirements and timings are realistic
  • I have adapted the plan with my own knowledge of my students
  • I could deliver this lesson confidently without the AI output if needed
