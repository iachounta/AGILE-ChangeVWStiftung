Using generative AI for lesson planning

Instruction sheet #03

A step-by-step guide for teachers: how to use AI tools to support — not replace — your lesson planning process, with a built-in prompt builder.

School level
K-12 (all levels)
Time needed
15 – 30 min
Prerequisite
Sheet #01 completed
1
Before you open any AI tool ▼
Write your learning objective first. In one sentence, complete: "By the end of this lesson, students will be able to…" Do this before opening the AI tool. The objective guides everything — the AI supports your plan, not the other way around.
Have your context ready. Note down: subject, grade or year group, lesson duration, any curricular constraints, and relevant prior knowledge of your students. The more specific your context, the more useful the AI output.
Confirm your tool is approved. Check your school's approved tool list (see Sheet #01). Do not enter student names, photos, or any identifying information into the tool.
Tip

If you cannot clearly state your learning objective before using the AI, the lesson plan it produces will likely be generic and require significant rework. Invest 5 minutes here — it saves 20 later.

2
Writing your prompt — and a builder to help ▼
A good prompt has three parts: (1) role — tell the AI it is helping a teacher; (2) task — what you want it to produce; (3) context — subject, grade, duration, and your learning objective.
Example prompt structure:
"You are helping a [subject] teacher at [school level]. Create a [duration]-minute lesson plan on [topic] for [grade/age]. Learning objective: students will be able to [objective]. Include: [starter / main activity / exit ticket / other]."

Tip — iterating

If the first output is too generic, refine: "Make the starter activity more hands-on" or "Adapt this for students with lower reading levels" or "Add a formative assessment moment at the midpoint." Save prompts that work well for reuse.

3
Reviewing and adapting the output ▼
Never use AI output without review. AI-generated lesson plans may contain factual errors, culturally inappropriate content, or activities unsuitable for your students. You are responsible for everything that happens in your classroom.
Check curriculum alignment. Does the activity match your national or school curriculum requirements? Is the cognitive level appropriate? Does the timing seem realistic for your class?
Add your professional knowledge. Insert your own examples, local references, and specific knowledge of your students. The AI has no knowledge of your class — you do. The best lesson plans blend AI efficiency with teacher expertise.
Ask the AI to explain its choices. If an activity seems unusual, prompt: "Why did you suggest this activity for this age group?" The answer helps you decide whether to keep, modify, or discard it.
Important

Do not copy AI output directly into official school documents or submit it as your own professional work without significant review and adaptation. Treat AI output as a first draft, not a finished product.

4
Pre-lesson checklist ▼
0 of 6 completed
  • I defined my learning objective before using the AI tool
  • No student personal data was entered into the AI tool
  • I reviewed all AI-generated content for factual accuracy
  • Activities align with my curriculum requirements and timings are realistic
  • I have adapted the plan with my own knowledge of my students
  • I could deliver this lesson confidently without the AI output if needed
